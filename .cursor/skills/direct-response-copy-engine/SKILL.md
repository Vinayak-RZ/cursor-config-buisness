# Direct-Response Copy Engine

This is a complete copywriting operator in one file. It does two jobs:

- **Generate.** Help you write a new ad from scratch, built on the principles that have worked for
  decades, tuned for how feeds behave in 2026.
- **Audit.** Take a draft you already have and pressure-test it line by line, then hand back a
  verdict, the fixes in priority order, and the single strongest rewrite.

It is built on three people who figured most of this out. Eugene Schwartz (where to start the
argument, and how fresh it has to sound). Michael Masterson and John Forde (one idea, and the
right opening). Harry Dry (whether the actual sentence earns its place). You do not need to have
read any of them. The method is all here.

**You need no prior setup.** You bring three things: what you sell, who you are talking to, and
the real proof you can point to. The skill brings everything else. It will never make up your
numbers, your customers, or your results. If something real is missing, it tells you, and you go
get it.

## How this file is organized

The operator is right here at the top. The full method sits below it in four parts. Read the
parts as you need them. The operator points you to the right part at the right moment.

- **Part 1, The Doctrine,** is the method. Read it once if you want the whole picture.
- **Part 2, Tactics for 2026,** is how the method applies to each format and platform.
- **Part 3, The Scorecard,** is the quality checklist. Audit mode runs it.
- **Part 4, Teardowns,** is famous ads taken apart, to calibrate your eye.

## Read first (the hard line)

These do not bend. They are why the copy works.

- **One idea per asset.** One dominant idea, one reader, one promise, one action. If a line does
  not serve the one idea, it is the wrong line, not a weaker line.
- **Channel desire, do not manufacture it.** The want already exists in the reader. The job is to
  name it better than they can and connect it to the offer. You never start at the product.
- **Concrete beats abstract.** If they cannot see it, they will not remember it.
- **Every claim must be falsifiable.** Provably true or false. Point at proof, do not pile on
  adjectives.
- **Write only what a competitor could not sign.** If a rival could paste your line under their
  logo, it sells the category, not you.
- **Never fabricate the user's facts.** Proof, numbers, customers, and results come from the user.
  If they are not real, the claim gets softened to what is true. No invented stats, no invented
  testimonials, no invented audience.
- **Get it in two seconds.** In the place the reader will actually see it, in a moving feed, not on
  a clean page.

## Setup (do this for both modes)

There are no files to load. You get the context by asking. Ask these up front, accept short
answers, and never fill a gap with a guess. Flag anything missing and work around it honestly.

1. **What are you selling?** The offer in one or two plain sentences. What it is, who it is for,
   what it does for them.
2. **Who is the one reader?** The single most-likely buyer. One person, one situation, not "anyone
   who needs this." If they describe a crowd, help them pick the one face.
3. **What is the real proof?** Numbers, results, before-and-afters, named customers, a founder
   story, credentials. Whatever is true and pointable. Write down only what is real. Mark what is
   missing.
4. **Where is the reader in awareness?** Use the five stages in Part 1, section 2. If unsure, ask
   one or two diagnostic questions: do they know they have this problem, do they know solutions
   like yours exist, do they already know your product. Place them.
5. **How tired is the market?** Use the five sophistication stages in Part 1, section 3. Roughly:
   are plain claims still fresh here, or has this audience heard every promise and every mechanism
   already. Place it.
6. **Which asset, and which mode?** Short-form video, static, carousel, landing page, or caption.
   Generate or Audit. If they are not sure which mode, default to asking whether they have a draft
   already. Draft means Audit. Blank page means Generate.

If the user gives thin answers, say what is thin and proceed with what you have. Thin proof means
smaller, truer claims, not invented ones.

## Mode A: Generate

Build the ad up in order. Do not jump to a finished line. At each step give the user a tactical
prompt and one worked example in a throwaway niche, then have them supply the real version in
their own words and their own proof. The output is theirs and grounded, not yours and invented.

### Step 1: Lock the one idea

Find the single most compelling true thing this offer can say. Run it against the three checks
(Part 1, section 1): easy to understand, easy to believe, interesting or unique.

> Worked example (throwaway niche, a sleep supplement): "You are not stressed, you are missing
> magnesium, and that is fixable tonight." One idea. Believable. Not what every sleep brand says.

Have the user state their one idea in one sentence. Do not move on until it passes the three
checks. If it fails, work the idea, not the wording.

### Step 2: Pick the lead

Choose the opening shape from the six great leads (Part 1, section 5), matched to the awareness
stage from setup. Colder and more cynical, reach for Story, Problem-Solution, or Proclamation.
Warmer and ready, reach for Promise or Offer.

> Worked example (problem-aware, tired market): a Problem-Solution lead. "You lie down exhausted
> and your brain switches on. Every night. Here is what was actually going on."

Tell the user which lead you recommend and why, given their awareness and sophistication. Have
them pick.

### Step 3: Calibrate to the square

Take the one idea and recast the wording for the exact awareness x sophistication square (Part 1,
section 4). Same idea, right register. In a tired market, lead with the mechanism or the identity,
not the plain promise.

> Worked example (solution-aware, high sophistication): "Melatonin knocks you out and leaves you
> groggy. This works with the gap, not against your brain." Wins on the how, not the promise.

Have the user write their version of the idea for their square.

### Step 4: Write the hook

Build the opening line and, for video, the first frame. Make it pass the three line tests and the
two-second gate (Part 1, sections 6 and 7). For video and captions, follow the scroll-stop rules
in Part 2: open mid-claim or mid-moment, put the hook in on-screen text for silent autoplay, make
the visual and the words agree, cut any windup.

> Worked example: on-screen text frame one reads "I fixed my 3am wake-ups in a week." Spoken line
> matches. No "hey guys."

Have the user draft three hook options, flow the tap, then keep the one that stops the thumb.

### Step 5: Write the body

Lay out the beats for the format (Part 2, per-format playbooks). Keep the one idea. Open a loop
early and pay it off late. Add a re-hook every few beats so the reason to keep going never runs
out. Every claim points at real proof from setup. Cut any beat that does not advance the one idea.

> Worked example (short-form video body): name the pain, reveal the real cause, show the proof,
> name the contrast with what they tried, then the turn.

Walk the user beat by beat. For each beat, name the move and give one example shape, then have
them write the real line with their proof.

### Step 6: One clear action

End with a single ask, stated plainly. One step, one link. Cut every secondary ask.

> Worked example: "First bottle ships today. Sleep tonight or it is free." One action.

### Step 7: Score it before you ship

Run the finished piece through Part 3, The Scorecard. Fix any flagged item using its fix prompt. A
version they only "kind of like" is a sign to keep going, not to stop.

### Optional: spin variations

Hold the one idea constant and swap the lead or the format to make test variations. A Story lead
and a Proclamation lead on the same idea. A video cut and a static cut of the same claim. This
gives the user a small slate to test instead of one bet.

## Mode B: Audit

Take the user's draft exactly as written. Do not rewrite it yet. Diagnose, then prescribe.

### Step 1: Read it cold and name the one idea

Read the draft once, fast, the way the reader will. Then try to state its single idea in one
sentence. If you cannot, that is the first finding: the piece has no one idea, or has several. Say
so plainly.

### Step 2: Run the scorecard

Go through Part 3, The Scorecard, item by item. Mark each Pass or Flag. Be specific: quote the
exact line that fails and say which test it fails.

- The four core items first (Rule of One, awareness match, sophistication match, the three checks
  on the idea). A core flag means do not ship yet.
- Then the line tests on every important line (visualize, falsify, nobody else, two-second gate).
- Then the structure items (lead fit, hook, body, proof, one action).

### Step 3: Give the verdict

Say where it stands in plain terms, using the scorecard's scoring guide:
- Core item flagged: not ready, the foundation needs fixing first.
- Core passes, lines flagged: right idea, wording is not there yet.
- Close: tighten the named structure item.
- Mostly passing: ship and let results teach.

### Step 4: Prescribe fixes in priority order

List the fixes, most important first. For each, name the failing line, the test it failed, and the
specific move to fix it (from the scorecard fix prompts and the doctrine). Foundation before
wording, always. Do not hand back a vague "make it punchier." Hand back the exact move.

### Step 5: Give one strongest rewrite

Rewrite the single most important element, usually the hook or the one idea, to show what passing
looks like. Use only the user's real proof. If the rewrite needs a fact the user has not given,
stop and ask for it rather than inventing it. Offer the rewrite as a model of the move, and invite
them to write the final version in their own voice.

---

## Recommendations (close every session with these)

- **Tie the fixes to the format.** Point the user to the matching playbook in Part 2 for their
  asset, and give the two or three format-specific moves that matter most for them.
- **Name the one test it should pass.** Whatever was weakest, give the user the single check to run
  before they post: usually the two-second gate or the nobody-else test.
- **Suggest the next variation worth making.** One alternative lead or format on the same one idea,
  so they are testing, not guessing.

## Notes

- This skill needs no internet, no account, and no prior files. Everything it relies on is in this
  file.
- It will not invent proof, customers, audiences, or numbers. Missing real proof means a smaller,
  truer claim, never a fabricated one.
- It judges copy by one thing: how much it would sell. Not how clever it sounds, not how much it
  stirs up. If a line is confusing or divisive and still sells nothing, it failed.
- When in doubt about any principle, the full method is in Part 1 and the examples are in Part 4.

# Part 1: The Doctrine

The whole craft of direct-response copy, compressed to the moves that still decide whether an
ad works in 2026. Three people figured most of this out. Eugene Schwartz wrote Breakthrough
Advertising. Michael Masterson and John Forde wrote Great Leads. Harry Dry runs Marketing
Examples. Everything below is theirs, restated plainly and stripped to what you can use today.

Read this once. Then keep the scorecard nearby and write.

## 0. The one law underneath everything

Copy does not create desire. It channels desire that already exists.

This is Schwartz, and it is the thing most people get backwards. You cannot force a person to
want something. You cannot argue a stranger into a craving. The want is already in them. Your
job is to find the want, name it better than they can name it themselves, and connect it to the
thing you sell.

So you never start at your product. You start at the person and the want they already carry.
The product is the bridge to a place they already want to go.

Practical version: before you write a word, finish this sentence in their language, not yours.
"They already want ______, they just do not believe ______ can get them there." The first blank
is the desire you channel. The second is the job of the copy.

## 1. The Rule of One (Masterson)

One idea. One reader. One promise. One action.

Weak copy stacks. It lists six benefits, three offers, and four reasons, then hopes one of them
lands. Masterson calls this tossed-salad copy. It feels safe because you covered everything. It
fails because it asked the reader to assemble the argument themselves, and they will not.

Strong copy is built on a single dominant idea and bends everything else toward it. When Agora
studied its hundred best-performing promotions of all time, the overwhelming majority were
built on one idea, not many.

**One idea.** The single most compelling thing you could say. Every story, stat, and bullet
exists only to prove this one thing. If a line does not serve the one idea, it is not a weaker
line, it is the wrong line. Cut it.

**One reader.** You are writing to a single person, not a market. Picture them. Most-likely
buyer, one face, one situation. "You" means them, not everyone.

**One promise.** The transformation, stated once and stated clearly. Not five outcomes. The one
they would trade money for.

**One action.** One thing to do next. One link, one ask. A second option is not generosity, it
is a fork in the road, and a forked reader leaves.

### The test for your one idea

A strong one idea passes three checks. Masterson's words, my labels:

1. **Easy to understand.** They get it in one read, no decoder ring. If you have to explain the
   idea, it is not the idea yet.
2. **Easy to believe.** It fits what they already suspect is true. A claim that outruns their
   belief gets rejected, no matter how good the offer.
3. **Interesting or unique.** It says something they have not heard in those words. If a
   competitor could run the same line, it is not yet yours.

Three yeses and you have a spine to build on. Any no and you keep working the idea, not the
sentences around it.

The discipline hurts on purpose. Picking one forces you to decide what actually matters. The
hard part of copy is not the writing. It is finding the one idea. Once you have it, the rest
falls out of it almost on its own.

## 2. Awareness: where the argument starts (Schwartz)

You cannot start a race in the middle. Schwartz says every prospect sits at a stage of
awareness, and the stage decides where your copy begins and how long it has to walk before it
can ask for anything. Start in the wrong place and the best sentence in the world bounces off.

The current attitude is the start line. The attitude you want is the finish line. Awareness
tells you how far apart those two are.

**The five stages, coldest to warmest:**

1. **Unaware.** They do not know they have the problem, or will not admit it. You cannot pitch.
   You lead with a story, an identity, or a curiosity that gets them to feel the problem is
   theirs. No offer yet. The whole job is to wake the problem up.

2. **Problem-aware.** They feel the pain but do not know solutions exist, or do not believe any
   work. Name the problem better than they can. Make them feel understood and a little relieved
   that someone finally said it. Only then point at a path.

3. **Solution-aware.** They know solutions like yours exist and they are comparing. They do not
   yet know about you, or do not see why you are different. Lead with the reason to believe your
   approach beats the others. Differentiate the mechanism.

4. **Product-aware.** They know your product. They are weighing it. Handle the specific
   objections, stack the proof, lower the risk. Tell them why now and why you.

5. **Most-aware.** They already want it. They need a reason to act today. Lead with the offer,
   the deadline, the guarantee, the simple instruction. Get out of the way.

**The rule:** the colder the stage, the longer you delay the pitch and the more you lead with
the problem, the story, or the identity. The warmer the stage, the faster you can get to the
offer. Cold traffic on social is usually unaware or problem-aware. That is why a hard pitch to
a cold feed dies, and a story that names a private frustration travels.

## 3. Sophistication: how tired the market is (Schwartz)

Awareness is about the reader. Sophistication is about the market. It asks one question: how
many claims like yours has this audience already heard?

The more they have heard, the more your plain claim sounds like noise. Sophistication tells you
how hard you have to work to be believed and to be new. Five stages:

1. **Stage one, new market.** No one has made this claim before. Be direct. State the claim
   flat and big. "Lose weight." First mover wins on the plain promise.

2. **Stage two, claims are working.** Competitors copy the claim. Now you enlarge it. Bigger,
   faster, more specific than theirs. "Lose weight in 30 days without giving up bread."

3. **Stage three, the market has heard every claim.** Promises stop working because everyone
   promises. Now you lead with the mechanism, the how. The unique reason it works. "A new fat-
   burning compound your body already makes." The mechanism becomes the new thing to believe.

4. **Stage four, mechanisms are tired too.** Competitors copied the mechanism. Now you enlarge
   the mechanism. Make it easier, faster, more elegant than the rival mechanism. "The compound,
   now in a once-a-day form, no diet required."

5. **Stage five, the market is exhausted and cynical.** They have heard the claims and the
   mechanisms and stopped believing all of it. Promises and mechanisms are spent. Now you lead
   with identity, story, and experience. You sell who they are and how it feels, not what it
   does. You become the brand they trust because it sounds like them. Most crowded niches on
   social in 2026 sit here. This is why a founder story or a "this is for people like us" angle
   often beats another feature claim.

**The rule:** match your lead to the stage. Selling a plain promise into a stage-five market
makes you invisible. Match it, then push one notch past where the market sits, and you sound
fresh instead of late.

## 4. The awareness x sophistication grid

Awareness tells you where to start. Sophistication tells you how to say it so it still lands.
Run both before you write. The same core idea gets recast depending on the square you are in.

Worked example. One product: a sleep supplement. One idea: "fall asleep faster without
grogginess." Watch it move.

- **Problem-aware, low sophistication:** name the pain plainly and promise the fix. "Still wired
  at 1am? This helps you fall asleep in 20 minutes." The plain claim is fresh because the market
  has not heard it to death.

- **Problem-aware, high sophistication:** the plain claim is dead, so lead with the mechanism or
  the reframe. "It is not your stress keeping you up, it is a magnesium gap, and here is the form
  your body actually absorbs." Same pain, but now you point at a cause and a how.

- **Solution-aware, high sophistication:** they have tried melatonin and it left them foggy. Lead
  with the differentiated mechanism and the contrast. "Melatonin knocks you out and leaves you
  groggy. This works with the gap, not against your brain. No hangover." You win on the how, not
  the promise.

- **Most-aware, any sophistication:** they already want it. Lead with offer and ease. "First
  bottle, ships today, sleep tonight or it is free."

The move you are practicing: hold the one idea constant, change the lead to fit the square. Most
failing ads are a right idea aimed at the wrong square.

## 5. The six Great Leads (Masterson and Forde)

The lead is the opening. The first few seconds, the first lines, the first frame. It does one
job: earn the next moment. Masterson and Forde found that almost every strong lead is one of six
shapes, and which one you pick is governed by awareness. Warmer prospects tolerate a faster,
more direct lead. Colder prospects need an indirect one that earns the right to pitch.

Direct leads (for warmer prospects: product-aware, most-aware):

1. **The Offer.** Open with the deal itself. The product, the price, the terms, the guarantee.
   Works only when they already know and want the thing. "First bottle free, you only cover
   shipping." Coldest possible audience for this: most-aware.

2. **The Promise.** Open with the single biggest benefit, stated boldly. "Sleep through the
   night by Friday." Good for product-aware and warm solution-aware, where the benefit alone is
   still enough to stop them.

Indirect leads (for colder prospects: unaware, problem-aware, solution-aware):

3. **The Problem-Solution.** Open inside the pain, agitate it until it is undeniable, then turn
   toward the way out. The workhorse of cold social traffic. "You lie down exhausted and your
   brain switches on. Again. Here is why that happens, and what finally stopped it for me."

4. **The Big Secret.** Open with the promise of a specific, hidden thing they do not know.
   Curiosity is the engine. "There is a reason you wake up at 3am every night, and it is not
   stress." You must pay off the secret, or it reads as a trick.

5. **The Proclamation.** Open with a bold, almost contrarian claim or prediction that makes them
   stop and decide if they agree. "Melatonin is quietly wrecking your sleep." It picks a fight
   with the common belief, which earns attention in a tired market.

6. **The Story.** Open in a specific moment, a person, a scene. The most disarming lead for the
   coldest and most cynical audiences, because it does not feel like an ad. "It was 4am and I was
   googling 'why can't I sleep' for the hundredth night in a row." Story is your stage-five and
   unaware tool.

**The rule of thumb:** the colder and more cynical the audience, the more indirect the lead.
Cold feed, exhausted market, you reach for Story, Problem-Solution, or Proclamation. Warm and
ready, you reach for Promise or Offer. Picking the wrong lead for the temperature is the most
common reason a good idea gets scrolled past.

## 6. Harry Dry's three tests (run on every line)

This is the quality gate. Schwartz and Masterson tell you what to say and where to start. Dry
tells you whether the actual sentence earns its place. Run all three on every important line:
the hook, the promise, the headline, the bullets, the CTA.

### Test 1: Can I visualize it?

If they cannot see it, they will not remember it. Concrete beats abstract every time. "A better
way" evaporates. "A charging pitbull" sticks because you can see it coming at you. You cannot
drop "a better way" on your foot. You can drop a leg of lamb.

**The fix, the zoom-in.** Take the abstract word. Write it at the top of a page. Ask "what do I
actually mean," and rewrite it more specifically. Repeat until you hit something you can see.
"Regain fitness" zooms to "get off the couch" zooms to "run 5K." That last one is concrete. You
can picture it. (That is literally how Couch to 5K got its name, and that name is why people
remember it.)

Abstract is intangible. Concrete is a thing in the world. Always push down the ladder toward the
thing.

### Test 2: Can I falsify it?

Write things that are provably true or false. A falsifiable claim makes the reader sit up,
because you have put your head on the chopping block. "He is intelligent" is air. "He reads on
the tube" is true or false, so the reader leans in.

**The fix, do not talk, only point.** Get off the adjective trail. Instead of describing, point
at evidence. Do not say the investment is great, point at the 50-year chart. Do not say he is
good-looking, say he looks like Ryan Gosling. Do not say "premium," point at the thing that
makes it premium. Pointing takes more work, which is exactly why it is more convincing. Anyone
can type an adjective. Only the real thing has specifics to point at.

If a line cannot be true or false, it is decoration. Replace it with something you can point to.

### Test 3: Can nobody else say it?

Never write a line a competitor could sign. If a rival could paste your sentence under their
logo, it is not selling you, it is selling the category. "Change an entire industry" could be
any recruiter. "Worn by supermodels in London and dads in Ohio" could only be New Balance,
because it is bespoke and true.

This test forces you deeper into what you actually sell. The only way to write something nobody
else can say is to know something specific and true about your thing that no one else can
honestly claim.

**Scoring.** Three yeses, you are onto something. Three nos, you have written rubbish, no matter
how it reads. Most lines fail at least one test on the first pass. That is normal. You rewrite
until they pass.

## 7. The instant-clarity gate (Dry)

One Mississippi, two Mississippi. If the reader does not get it inside two seconds, it failed.
This is not the comprehension of a careful reader at a desk. It is a thumb moving past a feed, a
billboard at 60mph, a glance at a sign while walking to the beach. Form follows function: figure
out the one thing they need in that instant, and deliver only that.

Two notes that save a lot of arguing with yourself:

- **Test it in context, not in isolation.** A line looks great alone on a clean page. Drop it
  into the real environment, surrounded by other ads, other posts, a moving feed, and ask again:
  does it stop the thumb. Write and review where the reader will actually see it, at the size
  they will see it.

- **"Not everyone gets it" can be fine.** If a line speaks precisely to your reader and a
  stranger outside the audience does not get it, that is often a feature, not a bug. It signals
  you are talking to someone specific. The failure is a line that everyone reads and no one
  remembers.

## 8. How a line actually gets built (the process)

You do not write the finished line. You build up to it. Dry's own process, generalized:

1. **Start from a seed.** A true fact, a tension, a stat, a real moment. Write it down rough.
   "The difference between 1% and 2% is not 1%, it is 100%." It is not the ad yet. It is the
   seed.

2. **Find the conflict.** Draw a line down the page. On each side, write two things that
   oppose. Before and after, them and you, the old way and the new way, what they fear and what
   they want. Conflict is what makes a flat line interesting. Three kinds of opposition to reach
   for: a different approach, a different belief, or a named competitor.

3. **Flow the tap first, judge later.** Spit out bad versions on purpose. The first ideas are
   the muddy water in the pipe. Keep going and it runs clear. Do not edit while you generate.
   Generate, then cut.

4. **Cut toward concrete and short.** Each pass, push abstract to concrete, long to short, and
   pull out any rhythm or repetition that is already there. Shorter is usually better. Parallel
   structure ("tougher than an F-150, faster than a 911") is usually better than a list.

5. **Get a second set of eyes.** After hours in it, you cannot tell good from bad anymore. Send
   the top two or three versions to someone whose taste you trust. Watch which one they react to,
   not which one they say is "fine."

6. **Bleed the ink dry.** A version you "kind of like" is a signal to keep going, not to stop.
   The good one is usually a few rewrites past the one you settled for. Twenty-plus passes on a
   single important line is normal, not excessive.

The deeper point: this is not trickery and it is not decoration. It is the craft of saying one
true thing so clearly and so specifically that a stranger gets it in two seconds and remembers
it. Judge an ad by one thing only: how much it sells.

## 9. The whole doctrine on one breath

- Find the desire that already exists. Channel it, do not manufacture it.
- Pick one idea. Easy, believable, new. Bend everything toward it.
- Start where they are. Awareness sets the start line.
- Say it fresh enough for how tired the market is. Sophistication sets the register.
- Open with the right lead for the temperature. Cold gets story, warm gets the offer.
- Make every important line pass three tests: visualize, falsify, nobody else.
- Get it in two seconds or rewrite it.
- Build up to the line. Do not expect to write it clean the first time.

# Part 2: Tactics for 2026 Digital Media

The doctrine does not change. The surface it runs on does. Schwartz wrote for direct mail.
Dry teaches with billboards. The principles are the same, but a thumb on a phone behaves
differently from a reader at a kitchen table. This part translates the doctrine to the formats
you actually ship in 2026: short-form video, statics, carousels, landing pages, and captions.

Everything here is generic. Plug in your own offer, audience, and proof.

## The three things every platform now rewards

1. **The first second.** On a feed, the default is to keep scrolling. You are not competing with
   the other ad, you are competing with the next post. The first frame and the first spoken or
   written line decide everything. This is the lead from the doctrine, compressed to about one
   second.

2. **Retention, not just attention.** Stopping the scroll is half the job. Holding it is the
   other half. Platforms push what people watch to the end and rewatch. So you build the body to
   keep pulling, not just the hook to stop.

3. **Native over polished.** A clip that looks like a post outperforms a clip that looks like a
   commercial, in most feeds, most of the time. The moment it smells like an ad, the guard goes
   up. Sound like a person, not a brand.

## Two numbers worth knowing

You do not need a media-buying degree, but two diagnostics tell you where a video ad is breaking.

- **Hook rate.** Roughly, the share of people who keep watching past the first few seconds
  instead of scrolling. A low hook rate means your lead failed. Fix the first frame and the first
  line, not the offer.

- **Hold rate.** Roughly, the share who make it to or near the end. A healthy hook rate with a
  weak hold rate means you stopped them but the body sagged. Fix the middle: add a re-hook, cut
  the slow part, raise the stakes.

Read it like a funnel. People leave at the hook or they leave in the body. Diagnose which, then
fix that specific thing.

## Scroll-stop hooks (the first second)

The hook is the lead from the doctrine, built for a feed. Make it pass Dry's three tests and the
two-second gate, then make it survive a moving thumb.

What stops a thumb:

- **A concrete, falsifiable opening, not a windup.** "I lost 40 pounds and kept my Friday pizza"
  beats "Let me tell you about my weight loss journey." No throat-clearing. Open mid-action or
  mid-claim.
- **Visual and verbal hook must agree.** What is on screen in frame one should match what is
  said or written in line one. If you say "look at this," there must be a this. A mismatch breaks
  the two-second gate.
- **Text on screen, because sound is off.** Assume the video autoplays silent. The opening line
  must be readable on screen in the first frame. If the hook only exists in the audio, most
  people never hear it.
- **Pattern interrupt that is relevant.** A jarring visual buys a beat of attention, but only
  pays off if it ties to the idea. A random loud thing gets a scroll a half-second later.

Match the hook shape to awareness (see Part 1, sections 2 and 5):
- Unaware or cold and cynical: open with a story moment or a proclamation.
- Problem-aware: open by naming the pain better than they can.
- Solution-aware: open with the contrast that makes your way different.
- Product-aware or most-aware: open with the promise or the offer.

## Retention and re-hooks (the body)

Once they are watching, your job is to keep reopening curiosity before it closes. A re-hook is a
small new question or tension dropped in every few seconds so the reason to keep watching never
runs out.

- **Open a loop early, close it late.** Tease the payoff up front ("the third one is the one
  nobody does"), deliver it near the end. The open loop is the rope that pulls them through.
- **Earn each segment.** Every few seconds, give a reason the next few seconds matter. A new
  twist, a "but here is the problem," a specific number, a turn in the story.
- **Cut the dead air.** If a beat does not advance the one idea or reopen curiosity, remove it.
  In short form, a slow second is a lost viewer.
- **Keep one idea.** Rule of One still rules. A video that tries to make three points loses the
  thread and the viewer. One idea, proven three ways, beats three ideas proven once.

## Per-format playbooks

### Short-form video (Reels, TikTok, Shorts, in-feed video ads)

The dominant format and the one most governed by hook rate and hold rate.

- Structure: **lead (1s) -> body that re-hooks (the middle) -> one clear ask (end).**
- Pick the lead by temperature. Cold feed usually means Story, Problem-Solution, or
  Proclamation, not Offer.
- Burn the hook into on-screen text in frame one. Silent autoplay is the default.
- One idea per video. If you have three ideas, make three videos.
- Native look. Shot on a phone, real person, real room, usually beats a studio spot.
- End with one action, said plainly. Not "link in bio and follow and comment." One thing.

### Static image ads

One image, very little text, a glance of attention. This is closest to Dry's billboard.

- One idea, one line, instant. The two-second gate is brutal here. If it needs a paragraph, it
  is the wrong format.
- The visual and the line must agree and reinforce. Picture points at the claim, claim names the
  picture.
- Falsifiable beats clever. Point at the proof in the image (the chart, the before, the real
  thing) rather than describing a feeling.
- Test it small and in-feed, surrounded by other posts, not full-screen on a clean canvas.

### Carousels (multi-slide image or document posts)

A sequence you swipe. The structure is the persuasion structure, one beat per slide.

- **Slide one is the hook and does only one job: earn the swipe.** Curiosity, a bold claim, a
  named pain. If slide one does not promise a payoff, no one sees slide two.
- One idea across the whole carousel. Each slide is one sub-point that proves the single idea, in
  order. This is the Rule of One applied to a sequence.
- Open a loop on slide one, pay it off on the last slide.
- Last slide is the single ask.
- Keep each slide to one readable thought. A crowded slide gets swiped past.

### Landing pages

Where the click goes to decide. More room, same rules.

- Match the page to the ad. The promise that got the click must be the first thing on the page.
  A mismatch between ad and page is the fastest way to lose the visitor you paid for.
- The lead at the top follows the six-leads logic, sized to where the traffic is in awareness.
  Cold traffic needs the problem or the story first. Warm traffic can see the offer first.
- One idea, one promise, one primary action. Every section proves the one idea. Remove anything
  that introduces a second idea or a second ask.
- Point, do not talk. Real proof, specific numbers, real screenshots and faces, not adjectives.
- Falsifiable claims throughout. "Used by 12,000 people" beats "loved by many."

### Captions and written posts

The copy under a video or image, or a standalone text post.

- The first line is the hook, because the platform truncates the rest behind "more." Earn the
  expand with line one.
- Short lines, white space, one idea. A wall of text is a scroll.
- Write it where the reader will read it, at the width they will read it, so you see the line
  breaks as they will.
- One ask at the end.

## A few traps specific to 2026 feeds

- **Do not bury the hook in a slow intro.** "Hey guys, so today I wanted to talk about" is a
  scroll. Cut everything before the actual hook.
- **Do not write for sound-on.** Most people watch silent. If the key line is only spoken, it is
  invisible. Put it on screen.
- **Do not let it look like an ad too soon.** The longer it reads as content, the longer they
  stay. Earn the pitch.
- **Do not stack CTAs.** Every extra ask lowers the odds of the one that matters.
- **Do not confuse engagement with selling.** A line can get comments because it is confusing or
  divisive and still sell nothing. Judge by what it sells, not what it stirs.

# Part 3: The Scorecard

The quality gate for any piece of copy. Run it on a draft and it tells you what is wrong and what
to do about it. This is what Audit mode uses. You can also keep it open while you write and score
your own work before anyone else sees it.

How to use it: go item by item. Mark each Pass or Flag. For every Flag, the fix prompt tells you
the move to make. A draft is ready when the four core items pass and most of the rest do too.
Nothing real passes every item on the first try.

## The four core items (a Flag here means do not ship yet)

### 1. Rule of One
**Check:** Is there exactly one dominant idea, one reader, one promise, one action? Can you say
the single idea out loud in one sentence?
**Flag if:** the piece makes several arguments, targets "everyone," promises multiple unrelated
outcomes, or offers more than one next step.
**Fix prompt:** Name the single most compelling idea. Cut or demote every line that does not prove
it. Reduce to one promise and one ask. If you cannot name the one idea, you have not found it yet.

### 2. Awareness match
**Check:** Does the opening start where the reader actually is? (Unaware, problem-aware,
solution-aware, product-aware, most-aware.)
**Flag if:** it pitches the offer to a cold or problem-aware reader, or slow-walks a most-aware
reader who is ready to buy.
**Fix prompt:** Identify the reader's stage. Move the start line to match. Colder means lead with
problem, story, or identity and delay the pitch. Warmer means get to the promise or offer faster.

### 3. Sophistication match
**Check:** Is the claim fresh enough for how many similar claims this market has already heard?
**Flag if:** it states a plain promise into a market that has heard that promise a thousand times,
so it sounds like everyone else.
**Fix prompt:** Gauge how tired the market is. If plain claims are dead, lead with the mechanism
(the how) or, in an exhausted market, with identity and story. Push one notch past where the
market sits.

### 4. The one idea passes the three checks
**Check:** Is the core idea easy to understand, easy to believe, and interesting or unique?
**Flag if:** it needs explaining, outruns what the reader will believe, or could have been said by
any competitor.
**Fix prompt:** Simplify until it lands in one read. Pull the claim back to something believable,
or add the proof that makes the big claim credible. Sharpen until it is something only you can say.

## The line-level tests (run on every important line)

Apply these to the hook, the promise, the headline, each bullet, and the CTA.

### 5. Can I visualize it?
**Check:** Can the reader see it? Is it a concrete thing, not an abstraction?
**Flag if:** the line uses vague nouns like "solution," "results," "better way," "transformation."
**Fix prompt:** Run the zoom-in. Write the abstract word, ask "what do I actually mean," rewrite
more specifically, repeat until you hit a thing you can picture. "Get fit" becomes "run 5K."

### 6. Can I falsify it?
**Check:** Is the line provably true or false? Could you point at evidence for it?
**Flag if:** it is an adjective parade ("premium, powerful, life-changing") with nothing to point
at.
**Fix prompt:** Do not talk, point. Replace the adjective with the specific fact, number, chart,
screenshot, or comparison that earns it. "Fast" becomes "ready in 11 seconds."

### 7. Can nobody else say it?
**Check:** Would the line still be true and specific if a competitor pasted it under their logo?
**Flag if:** a rival could sign it without lying. It sells the category, not you.
**Fix prompt:** Find the specific, true thing only you can claim, your real mechanism, your real
proof, your real story, and build the line on that.

### 8. The two-second gate
**Check:** Does the reader get it in two seconds, in the place they will actually see it?
**Flag if:** it needs a careful read, or only works full-screen on a clean page but dies in a busy
feed.
**Fix prompt:** Cut to the one thing they need in that instant. Shorten. Put the key line in
on-screen text. Test it small, surrounded by other posts.

## The structure items

### 9. Lead fit
**Check:** Is the opening one of the six leads, and is it the right one for the temperature?
(Offer, Promise, Problem-Solution, Big Secret, Proclamation, Story.)
**Flag if:** a cold audience gets a direct Offer or Promise lead, or a hot audience gets a long
indirect story before the ask.
**Fix prompt:** Cold and cynical, reach for Story, Problem-Solution, or Proclamation. Warm and
ready, reach for Promise or Offer. Rebuild the opening as that shape.

### 10. The hook earns the next moment
**Check:** Does the first line or first frame make continuing irresistible? On video, is the hook
on screen in frame one for silent autoplay?
**Flag if:** it opens with a windup, a greeting, or context before the actual hook. Or the hook
lives only in the audio.
**Fix prompt:** Delete everything before the real hook. Open mid-claim or mid-moment. Put the hook
in on-screen text.

### 11. The body holds (for anything longer than one line)
**Check:** Does each segment reopen curiosity or raise the stakes so they keep going?
**Flag if:** the middle sags, repeats, or coasts after the hook.
**Fix prompt:** Add a re-hook every few beats. Open a loop early and pay it off late. Cut any beat
that does not advance the one idea.

### 12. Proof, not adjectives
**Check:** Are claims backed by specific, real proof rather than described with feeling words?
**Flag if:** it leans on "amazing," "proven," "trusted," with nothing concrete behind them.
**Fix prompt:** Point at the real thing. Specific numbers, real names and faces, screenshots,
before and after. Never invent proof. If the proof does not exist, soften the claim to what is
true.

### 13. One clear action
**Check:** Is there exactly one next step, stated plainly?
**Flag if:** there are multiple asks, or the ask is vague.
**Fix prompt:** Pick the single most important action. Cut the rest. State it in plain words.

## Scoring the whole piece

- **Any core item (1 to 4) flagged:** not ready. Fix the foundation before touching the wording.
- **Core items pass, several line tests flagged:** the idea is right, the writing is not there
  yet. Rewrite the flagged lines using their fix prompts.
- **Core passes, line tests mostly pass, a structure item flags:** close. Tighten the lead, body,
  proof, or CTA as flagged.
- **Mostly passing with one or two soft flags:** ship it, then let the results teach you. The
  honest signal is what it sells, not what it scores.

A reminder from the doctrine: a version you only "kind of like" is a sign to keep going. The
strong version is usually a few rewrites past the one you settled for.

# Part 4: Teardowns

The doctrine, shown working. Each of these is a famous, public ad line. Under each one is why it
lands, mapped to the tests in the doctrine. Read them to calibrate your eye. When your own line
stalls, come back here and ask which move it is missing.

These are well-known public examples used for teaching. None of them are yours. The point is the
pattern, not the line.

## "New Balance. Worn by supermodels in London and dads in Ohio."

**Why it works.**
- **Visualize:** you can see both people instantly. A supermodel on a London street, a dad at a
  barbecue in Ohio, both in New Balance. Two concrete pictures, no abstraction.
- **Falsify:** it is literally true. Supermodels have worn them, dads do wear them. You could
  check.
- **Nobody else:** Prada cannot say this. Reebok cannot say this. The split between high fashion
  and suburban dad is bespoke to this one brand. A competitor could not sign it.
- **One idea:** universal appeal, said one way. Not "comfortable, stylish, versatile, popular."
  One idea, dramatized.

**The lesson:** the juxtaposition does the work. Two specific, true, opposite pictures that only
this brand can claim.

## Volvo. "Your car has five numbers on the speedometer. A Volvo has six."

**Why it works.**
- **Falsify and point:** it does not say "Volvo is safer" or "Volvo is built with confidence." It
  points at a thing you can verify by sitting in the car and counting the dials. Pure pointing,
  no adjectives.
- **Nobody else:** the only way to make this claim is to actually have the higher number on the
  speedometer. It is structurally un-copyable.
- **Visualize:** you picture the speedometer. The proof is visual and the line is visual, and they
  agree.

**The lesson:** when you want to claim something abstract like confidence, find the concrete,
checkable detail that proves it, and point at that instead of naming the feeling.

## Apple iPod. "1,000 songs in your pocket."

**Why it works.**
- **One idea, the right one:** competitors led with specs, gigabytes and megahertz. Apple threw
  all of it out for the single thing the buyer actually wanted, the whole music library, carried
  anywhere.
- **Visualize:** "in your pocket" is a picture. The literal truth was "1,000 songs in your media
  player," which is abstract and forgettable. They swapped the abstract word for the concrete one.
- **Two-second gate:** you get it instantly. No decoding.

**The lesson:** find the one benefit that matters, then say it in the most concrete, physical words
you can, even if a more literal phrasing would be "more accurate." Pocket beats media player.

## The Economist. "I never read The Economist." Management trainee. Aged 42.

**Why it works.**
- **Falsify and the implied story:** it does not claim "The Economist makes you successful." It
  points. A 42-year-old still a trainee, who never reads it. You assemble the conclusion yourself,
  which is why you believe it. You were not told, you decided.
- **One idea:** read this and you will not be stuck. Said sideways, through one character, in a few
  words.
- **Visualize:** you see the man. The trainee at 42 is a specific, slightly painful picture.

**The lesson:** the strongest claims are often the ones you let the reader reach on their own. Point
at the situation, let them draw the line.

## De Beers. "How can you make two months' salary last forever? The diamond engagement ring."

**Why it works.**
- **One idea, with built-in tension:** two months of salary set against forever. It should not
  work, money runs out, and that contradiction makes you stop. The tension is the hook.
- **Nobody else and the category invention:** this campaign more or less created the rule that an
  engagement ring is a diamond. It claimed a meaning no competing stone had claimed.
- **Believable through reframing:** it reframes an expense as permanence, which makes a large price
  feel like a value.

**The lesson:** put two true things in tension. The contradiction earns attention, and resolving it
delivers the idea.

## Hinge. "The dating app designed to be deleted."

**Why it works.**
- **Nobody else:** no other app would claim to want you gone. It is structurally un-copyable
  because it cuts against the whole category's incentive, which is exactly why it is believable as
  a promise about their intent.
- **One idea:** this app is for people who want a real relationship, not endless swiping. Said in
  six words.
- **Implied story and the payoff:** "deleted" implies you met someone and no longer need it. The
  outcome the reader wants is baked into the line.
- **Sophistication, stage five:** in an exhausted dating-app market full of feature claims, it wins
  on identity and intent, not features.

**The lesson:** in a tired market, the winning line is often the one that takes the opposite
position to the whole category and means it.

## "Couch to 5K."

**Why it works.**
- **Visualize, the zoom-in made visible:** "get fit" is abstract. This names the exact start (the
  couch) and the exact finish (5K). Both are pictures. The name is the whole promise.
- **Believable:** the distance between the two points feels crossable. It does not promise a
  marathon. It promises the next, believable step.
- **Two-second gate:** the name alone tells you what it is and who it is for.

**The lesson:** name the concrete before and the concrete after. Often the clearest promise is just
those two points, stated plainly.

## Snickers. "You're not you when you're hungry."

**Why it works.**
- **One idea, dramatized:** hunger makes you act unlike yourself. One thought, carried across every
  execution.
- **Visualize and falsify by demonstration:** the ads show a specific person turning into a diva or
  a grump until they eat. You see it, and it rings true from your own life.
- **Channels existing desire:** it does not manufacture a want. It names something everyone has
  felt, then attaches the bar to the fix.

**The lesson:** find the universal, true, slightly funny human moment your product resolves, then
dramatize that one moment rather than listing what the product is.

## How to use these on your own work

When a line of yours feels flat, run it against the patterns above:

- Is it abstract where the New Balance line is concrete? Zoom in.
- Are you describing where Volvo points? Find the checkable detail.
- Are you listing specs where the iPod gave one benefit? Cut to the one.
- Are you stating the conclusion where The Economist lets the reader reach it? Point, do not
  conclude.
- Could a competitor sign it, where Hinge could not? Find the un-copyable truth.

The pattern is always the same underneath: one idea, made concrete, provable, and impossible for
anyone else to say, delivered in two seconds.
